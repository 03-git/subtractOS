RECOGNIZE PHISHING EMAILS AND TEXTS

Steps:
1. CHECK THE SENDER: Look at the actual email address, not just the display name. Hover over it. "support@amaz0n-security.com" is not Amazon.
2. CHECK FOR URGENCY: "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours" / "Act now" / "Immediate action required" — legitimate companies rarely threaten you via email.
3. CHECK LINKS BEFORE CLICKING: Hover over any link (don't click). Does the URL match the company it claims to be? Look for misspellings, extra subdomains (e.g., paypal.com.scamsite.net).
4. CHECK FOR GENERIC GREETINGS: "Dear Customer" instead of your name. Legitimate companies usually know your name.
5. CHECK ATTACHMENTS: Unexpected attachments, especially .zip, .exe, .html, or Office docs "requiring macros" — do not open.
6. CHECK THE REQUEST: No legitimate company will ask for your password, SSN, or full credit card number via email or text.
7. WHEN IN DOUBT: Do not click anything in the message. Go directly to the company's website by typing the URL yourself, or call them using the number on your card/their official site.

Notes:
- Phishing texts (smishing) follow the same patterns: fake urgency, suspicious links, unknown senders pretending to be your bank or a delivery service.
- If you clicked a phishing link: change your password for that service immediately, enable 2FA, and monitor your accounts.
- Report phishing emails: forward to reportphishing@apwg.org. Report phishing texts: forward to 7726 (SPAM).
